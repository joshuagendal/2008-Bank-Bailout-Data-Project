2008 Bank Bailout Data Project

Hello, and welcome to my website! My name is Josh, and I'm not "just Joshin' you". During my undergrad years at Florida State University (go Noles!), I wrote several political science research papers and for these papers I had developed a data set on all of the members of the House of Representatives of the 110th Congress, with 40+ columns of information. Using the django-python web framework, I created this website as a way for people to interact with this data set, and to, hopefully, learn a thing or two in the process.

On this page, I will go into more detail about my research paper. But please, explore this website and make sure to sign up and create a user so you can rate members of the House of Representatives!

Project and Research Paper Background

The 2008 financial crisis was a trying time for the United States of America. Irresponsible, and some would argue illegal, mortgage and investing practices by large financial institutions beginning in the Clinton years resulted in the sub-prime mortgage crisis in 2008. Toxic investments in a troubled housing market had a malicious domino effect on the U.S., and world, economy. Investment banking firm Lehman Brothers was a truly pivotal domino to fall, declaring insolvency on September 15, 2008. This resulted in a financial panic in the stock market that had the potential to foster a bank run. The dominoes continued to fall to various financial markets, when the inevitability of the collapse of the credit market prompted government officials to take action.

In response to the financial crisis, Treasury Secretary Henry Paulson announced his Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP) on September 20. Known as the Paulson Proposal, TARP proposed that the Treasury would buy up to $700 billion of Mortgage Backed Securities owned by large financial institutions, in hopes of stabilizing financial markets by directly reducing the uncertainty in the financial market and banking system. Four days later, President Bush addressed the nation on primetime television to rally public support for Paulson’s Proposal.

In Congress, the TARP proposal became a bill called the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008 (EESA). The bill was taken up expeditiously for a vote in the House on September 29, but it failed 205-228. That same day, investors responded to the failed bailout, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average had its largest single-day point drop in its history . The media’s reporting was starting to become frightening, warning that people’s savings and 401k investments were at risk of being lost. After being passed in the Senate on October 1, the EESA was sent back to the lower chamber for a revote on October 3. This time, the EESA passed the House comfortably, 263-171. Between the first and second votes on the $700 billion bailout, 58 members of the House of Representatives changed their vote from “nay” to “yay.”

The research paper I wrote for my honors thesis in college, and whose data is the basis for this web app, tried to find statistical factors that may have caused 58 members of the House of Representatives to switch their vote from "no" to "yes" on the EESA. I looked at factors such as financial services Political Action Committee campaign contributions, ideology, bailout public opinion by district, and others.

Unfortunately, my data analysis for the project did not yield any deifnitive conclusions on what factors may have directly caused these members of the House of Representatives to switch their vote on this very important bill.
